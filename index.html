Interoperability maturity does not depend only on standards or digital infrastructure. It depends on how identity, governance, trust, medicines information, deployment and correction capacity reinforce one another in practice.

Technological progress is advancing faster than the evolution of many health systems. Significant delays remain in operational adoption, institutional redesign and the integration of interoperability into routine clinical protocols.

These delays also affect training. Physicians, pharmacists, biologists, nurses, allied health professionals and other health workers have not yet been systematically trained for an environment in which interoperable data and broader access to previously siloed information reshape clinical judgment, coordination and accountability.

As interoperability expands, access to information that was once reserved or difficult to obtain will help transform health education, professional roles and care practices. Over time, interoperability should become an integral part of clinical protocols, health-system design and workforce formation.
