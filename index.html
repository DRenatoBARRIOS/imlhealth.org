ID4D

A trust and inclusion lens for identity-enabled public infrastructure.

Interoperability maturity does not depend only on standards or digital infrastructure. It depends on how identity, governance, trust, medicines information, deployment and correction capacity reinforce one another in practice.

Technological progress is advancing faster than the evolution of many health systems. Significant delays remain in operational adoption, institutional redesign and the integration of interoperability into routine clinical protocols.

These delays also affect training. Physicians, pharmacists, biologists, nurses, allied health professionals and other health workers have not yet been systematically trained for an environment in which interoperable data and broader access to previously siloed information reshape clinical judgment, coordination and accountability.

As interoperability expands, access to information that was once reserved or difficult to obtain will help transform health education, professional roles and care practices. Over time, interoperability should become an integral part of clinical protocols, health-system design and workforce formation.

ID4D Framework

Positioning IML in alignment with the ID4D approach

IML can be aligned with ID4D by treating digital identity as an enabling public infrastructure layer for inclusive access, trusted service delivery, interoperability and accountable governance.

ID4D and health interoperability

The World Bank’s ID4D approach is not a medical terminology in itself. It is a framework for building inclusive, trusted and interoperable identification systems grounded in legal, organizational, semantic and technical interoperability.

In health, this matters because interoperability does not depend only on clinical standards. It also depends on the ability to identify individuals reliably, authenticate actors, define governance responsibilities, protect privacy and exchange data under a common trust framework.

Why this matters for IML

IML does not treat interoperability as a simple exchange of files or messages. It evaluates whether a system can create trusted continuity across persons, institutions, medicines information and care events.

In that perspective, ID4D helps explain why identity and governance are not secondary layers. They are part of the practical conditions that make interoperability usable, scalable and governable.

Proposed codification model

From visible identifier to unifying health codification

The proposed IML codification should not be understood as a simple identifier format. It is designed as a unifying reference layer that connects identities, care events, medicines, documents and institutional actors across the health system.

The visible identifier is only one component of a broader architecture that supports interoperability, governance, traceability and continuity of care.

Without such a unifying logic, health systems may exchange data technically while remaining fragmented in practice, with duplicated records, isolated medicines information and weak accountability across institutions.

Operational scope

What the codification is meant to structure

  • Person identity and patient continuity across services
  • Provider identity and accountability of clinical actions
  • Facility and institutional references across the care network
  • Medicines, prescriptions and dispensing events
  • Documents, laboratory results and care episodes
  • Longitudinal linkage between events, follow-up and governance
ID4D alignment

Why ID4D matters in this architecture

ID4D provides the enabling identity and trust layer that supports reliable identification, authentication and governed access control.

The proposed codification builds on that foundation to organise health-specific information in a coherent and interoperable way, linking identity with care events, medicines and institutional responsibilities.

It is therefore not a replacement for existing clinical terminologies. It is a transversal layer that makes different categories of health information more governable, interoperable and usable across institutional boundaries.

Evaluation and regulatory shifts

Evaluation and regulatory shifts in an interoperable environment

Health interoperability maturity directly affects clinical evaluation, trial design, long-term follow-up and regulatory decision-making. It enables a shift from narrow protocol-based evidence toward more comprehensive, longitudinal and real-world clinical understanding, including a regulatory transition toward structured long-term evaluation.

Core proposition

A mature interoperability and identity layer can make it possible to assemble a safeguarded, person-linked longitudinal view of clinically relevant information across prescriptions, dispensing events, laboratory results, care episodes, prior conditions and follow-up signals.

This does not mean collapsing all information into a single uncontrolled profile. It means enabling authorised, governed and proportionate access to the information needed to understand medicines use in real care pathways.

Regulatory shift

The larger point is that health systems are still at an early stage in their use of new technologies. What is visible today is important, but it likely represents only the first layer of what interoperable, identity-aware and clinically usable digital infrastructures could support over time.

Special note for pharmaceutical industry and regulatory authorities

The IML framework suggests that current clinical evaluation models can be extended beyond short-term, protocol-bound trials toward more continuous, longitudinal and real-world evaluation environments.

This implies the possibility of integrating authorised, identity-linked clinical data across time, settings and care pathways, enabling long-term follow-up, better understanding of delayed effects, and more robust safety and effectiveness analysis.

For regulatory agencies, this opens a pathway to complement traditional clinical trials with structured long-term evaluation capabilities, without weakening scientific rigor or governance safeguards.

Methodology

A framework that is simple to read and strong enough to defend

The IML methodology combines six assessment dimensions, a maturity score, country benchmarking and a trust-oriented view of digital identity.

The six assessment criteria

  • Criterion 1 · Normative framework
  • Criterion 2 · Technical interoperability
  • Criterion 3 · Identity and trust
  • Criterion 4 · Deployment
  • Criterion 5 · Security
  • Criterion 6 · Correction, recourse and feedback

Score explainer

The score is not a beauty contest. It is a structured reading of a country or institution’s ability to govern, operate, secure and scale interoperability.

A high score does not reflect technology alone. It also reflects governance discipline, trust services, operational uptake and visible correction capacity.

Proposed codification model

Rationale for a proposed unifying codification in health

The proposed codification is not designed to collapse all health information into a single universal code. It is designed to establish a coherent reference architecture through which person identity, provider identity, facility references, medicines information, documents and care events can be linked and interpreted consistently.

It therefore complements existing clinical terminologies rather than replacing them. Its role is to provide a transversal logic that makes different categories of health information more governable, interoperable and usable across institutional boundaries.

A mature health system should be able to connect who the person is, who provided the care, where the event occurred, which medicine or product was involved, which document or result was generated, and how that event should be interpreted within a larger care pathway.

Operational structure

What the proposed codification is meant to organize

  • Person identity and patient continuity across services
  • Provider identity and accountability of clinical actions
  • Facility and institutional references across the care network
  • Medicines, dispensing events and regulatory traceability
  • Documents, laboratory results and care episodes
  • Longitudinal linkage between events, follow-up and governance

Without this unifying logic, systems may exchange data technically while remaining fragmented in practice, with duplicated records, isolated medicines information and weak accountability across institutions.

Explanatory note on the proposed identity code

The proposed request-validated identity code is presented here as a methodological annex, not as a universal civil identifier. Its role is to show how identity can be structured for interoperability, research and regulated use while remaining compatible with purpose limitation and controlled linkage.

Visible format

IML1-S-DDMMYYYY-GEO4-SSSS-CC

Example: IML1-2-17041986-Q7L2-0421-58

Governance and integration envelope

The visible identifier should be linked to a controlled integration envelope that can include name elements, contact channels, local record numbers, national identifiers where permitted, consent state, research identifiers and linkage tables for matching or correction workflows.

  • Use the visible code only as a request-validated identifier, not as a universal public number propagated everywhere.
  • Keep other personal data in a controlled integration envelope rather than encoding them directly in the visible identifier.
  • The integration envelope can include name elements, contact channels, local medical-record numbers, national identifiers, consent state, study identifiers and linkage tables when legally justified.
