ID4D

A trust and inclusion lens for identity-enabled public infrastructure.

Interoperability maturity does not depend only on standards or digital infrastructure. It depends on how identity, governance, trust, medicines information, deployment and correction capacity reinforce one another in practice.

Technological progress is advancing faster than the evolution of many health systems. Significant delays remain in operational adoption, institutional redesign and the integration of interoperability into routine clinical protocols.

These delays also affect training. Physicians, pharmacists, biologists, nurses, allied health professionals and other health workers have not yet been systematically trained for an environment in which interoperable data and broader access to previously siloed information reshape clinical judgment, coordination and accountability.

As interoperability expands, access to information that was once reserved or difficult to obtain will help transform health education, professional roles and care practices. Over time, interoperability should become an integral part of clinical protocols, health-system design and workforce formation.

ID4D Framework

Positioning IML in alignment with the ID4D approach

IML can be aligned with ID4D by treating digital identity as an enabling public infrastructure layer for inclusive access, trusted service delivery, interoperability and accountable governance.

ID4D and the rationale for a unifying health codification

The World Bank’s ID4D approach is not a medical terminology in itself. It is a framework for building inclusive, trusted and interoperable identification systems grounded in legal, organizational, semantic and technical interoperability.

In health, this matters because interoperability does not depend only on clinical standards. It also depends on the ability to identify individuals reliably, authenticate actors, define governance responsibilities, protect privacy and exchange data under a common trust framework.

The rationale for a unifying codification in health is therefore not to collapse all information into one universal code, but to make person identity, provider identity, facility references, medicines information, documents and care events work within a coherent and governable structure.

In that sense, ID4D provides the enabling identity and governance layer, while sector-specific health terminologies, registries and standards provide the semantic layer. Together, they make it easier to reduce duplicate records, improve continuity of care, support safer medicines governance, strengthen accountability and enable more consistent interoperability across the health system.

Evaluation and regulatory shifts

Evaluation and regulatory shifts in an interoperable environment

Health interoperability maturity directly affects clinical evaluation, trial design, long-term follow-up and regulatory decision-making.

Core proposition

A mature interoperability and identity layer can make it possible to assemble a safeguarded, person-linked longitudinal view of clinically relevant information across prescriptions, dispensing events, laboratory results, care episodes, prior conditions and follow-up signals.

Regulatory shift

The larger point is that health systems are still at an early stage in their use of new technologies. What is visible today is important, but it likely represents only the first layer of what interoperable, identity-aware and clinically usable digital infrastructures could support over time.
